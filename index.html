Long Form


Ten-page Spread

I used Adobe Indesign to create a ten-page magazine spread about American Agriculture. I really liked the artistic map I found for the article's title page and decided to use that as the anchor for my theme. On the title page, I tried to replicate the font from the U.S. map for the article's title. I chose Miller Display as the body font because of its sharpness and readability, and Ivy Mode for my headers. My favorite page to design was page 6. I learned how to mold the text around the circle so that I could replicate a spread I saw in a home magazine when searching for inspiration. The most interesting page to write was the farmers quotes on page 4. I think the style of the "handwritten" notes helped the storytelling feel more personal and made those quotes stand out as real voices sharing their experiences. Overall, this is definitely the project I am most proud of from this class.

Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

I used visual rhetoric in my client design project by analyzing how color palettes and icons, such as the transition from earthy tones to muted blues, persuade specific audiences to view the town as an "outdoorsy" destination rather than a generic beach town. I used technology to structure the digital interviewing and transcription process. I made decisions about which tools, such as Canva, Photoshop Express, Illustrator, and InDesign, would be most efficient for each project. I upheld ethical standards by ensuring participant anonymity, obtaining informed consent before each interview recording, and using a controlled script to mitigate bias during data collection.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

For these designs, I focused on establishing a strong visual hierarchy by using consistent typography and color schemes. By prioritizing proximity and alignment with consistent left-aligned typography, the layouts maintain high legibility. The ten-page magazine spread and the social media client project both used lots of negative space compared to previous projects. This required more decisions about the placement of text and imagery so that the design’s hierarchy effectively guides the viewer’s eye. Also, the selection of a cohesive black, white, and beige palette (the beige color symbolizing the grains common in American agriculture) created a thematic unity. This purposeful repetition across the projects ensured a professional aesthetic and a singular, cohesive brand identity.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

I participated in several more workshops where I collaborated with peers and instructors to refine my design skills. During these workshops, I found that me and my peers usually had personal biases towards certain design versions or elements, thinking they were more aesthetic even if they were working against the goal and message of the design. When creating Mazanita's brand identity, I was really attatched to this pink/coral color that my classmates explained had no real connection to the town or my mission statement, instead adding a random feminine or playful style. I also had a peer who really wanted a black background for her logo, but no matter how she changed the fonts and icons, it still took away from the elegance she was trying to achieve. This showed me how important it is to get outside feedback, even early in the design process. These sessions provided valuable opportunities to receive feedback on my work and to learn from others' perspectives. The collaborative nature of these workshops helped me develop a broader understanding of design principles and their application in real-world scenarios.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

Revision was especially important in my design process for the client and UX design projects, where I conducted multiple rounds of user interviews to inform my final output. By integrating their feedback, I significantly improved the design's clarity and visual appeal. The most substantial transformations occurred within the ten-page spread. To move beyond a repetitive “magazine pattern,” a common critique during our workshop, I avoided the standard formula of top-aligned images and static text columns. While I retained that layout to some extent on page 3 (“The Corn Belt”), I reconfigured the remaining pages to embrace greater contrast, bolder creative risks, and a more intuitive visual hierarchy.

5. Learn to present your work.

I have learned how to prepare consise visuals and confident explanations so that I can explain my design choices to non-designers. Presenting my UX design project to the users in my study and the client project to the club's president were both tasks that challenged me to improve my communication skills. When presenting my UX design project to my peers, I had to summarize the over 40 minutes over interviewing I had conducted in just a couple slides. I was able to summarize the key insights to effectively communicate my design decisions and hold the audience's attention.
